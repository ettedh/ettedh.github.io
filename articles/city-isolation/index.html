City Isolation

The distances between cities.

City isolation

There is a concept in topography called peak isolation, which describes how far a mountain peak is, from another peak which is higher than itself. By analogy, you can describe cities as having a discrete location and population, and using similar algorithms to compute their city isolation. So, let’s define it:

The isolation of a city can be measured from the center of a city, to the center of the nearest city which has a higher population. City isolation is the distance along the surface between a city and the nearest city with a higher population.1 Kirmse 2017

Here I admit that the terminology can be confusing. In geography, prominence refers to the elevation difference between a peak, and the lowest contour that has it as a peak (Kirmse 2017). This term does not have a defined meaning in the discrete nature of city populations, except for cases within the same metro.

What is city population anyhow?

It should be noted that population is not a perfect analogy to elevation. Unlike elevation, human population is discrete, and so all population estimates are totals over an area. This makes the related metric of peak prominence undefined for population. Both of these problems could be solved by using population densities, but this has the problems of being way too varied over small areas and meaningless over large areas. Gravity model

The “gravity model” is a lot like the reverse of the isolation model: it compares one city with a system.

The model has two assumptions:

  1. Population is a good marker for attraction (pretty reasonable),
  2. Distance is also a good marker for attraction (in inverse).

It is so readily acceptable that the amount of outside trade which a city enjoys in any surrounding town is a direct function of the population of that city and an inverse function of the distance of the city from that town, that the general law needs no support.

The original formulation suggested recommends P/d^2, which matches conveniently to the formula for universal gravity.

Another interesting quirk with this is that it can show the direction towards a local, maximum population.
